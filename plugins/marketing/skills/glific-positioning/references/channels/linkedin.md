# Channel Playbook — LinkedIn

Two layers: **channel optimization** (how the feed rewards a post) and **messaging adaptation** (how Glific's positioning flexes for LinkedIn). Apply both. Always pull the actual benefit, proof, and quote from the backbone docs — this file governs *form*, not facts.

## When to use
Any LinkedIn post, carousel/document, or comment campaign — whether in the Glific org voice or a founder/team-member's personal voice.

## Channel optimization (how LinkedIn works)

- **The first two lines are everything.** The feed truncates at ~140–210 characters with a "…more." Earn the click with a consequence or a concrete moment — never with "Glific is…". No link, no hashtag, no name in the opener.
- **One idea per post.** The feed punishes posts that try to say three things. One benefit (or one differentiator), one proof, one takeaway.
- **Whitespace is formatting.** Short lines. One- or two-sentence paragraphs. Line breaks between beats. A wall of text dies.
- **Length:** ~900–1,500 characters for a text post. Long enough to tell one story, short enough to finish on the phone.
- **Specificity scrolls-stops.** A number (3,000 girls, 420,000 respondents) or a real quote in the first third earns the read.
- **One CTA, soft.** End with a question that invites a comment, or a single link in the first comment (not the body — body links suppress reach). Don't stack "Book a call + visit site + download."
- **Hashtags:** 3–5, specific (#NonprofitTech #WhatsAppForGood #NGOIndia), at the end, not sprinkled inline.
- **Carousels (documents)** are for the 4-step journey or a before/after story — one idea per slide, big type, the proof on the payoff slide.
- **Native > links.** Tell the whole story in-feed; the reader shouldn't need to leave to get the point.

## Messaging adaptation (how Glific shows up here)

- **Lead with the program reality and the benefit.** Open on a real situation a program lead would recognise, and land on the value — time and effort saved, or reach at scale. Glific is SaaS; the post sells the value, not "join a community."
- **Community is a differentiator you can feature — in proportion.** A post can be *about* the peer network, but most posts lead with the benefit and let community be the "why Glific." Don't make every post a belonging story.
- **Protagonist = the NGO/program lead, never Glific.** Glific should appear past the first third, as the guide.
- **Proof carries the post.** One sourced number or one verbatim quote does the persuading. The Civis 2,500→420,000 jump and TAP's 10,000+ students are the strongest scale proof; the MukkaMaar "it feels like I have a team" quote is the strongest community proof.
- **Sage register, even here.** Measured and explanatory. The hook is a concrete observation, not a theatrical reversal. No "the X was never the problem," no dramatic one-liners, no "what stayed with me was…".
- **Founder/personal voice** can be first-person and reflective, but still calm and grounded; **org voice** is warmer-institutional. Both keep the NGO first.
- **Sector-specific framing** outperforms generic — an education post for education followers, a civic-tech post for that audience.

## Post templates

**Template A — Problem → proof → reflection (the default).**
```
[Line 1–2: a concrete program reality, no Glific name, no theatrical hook]
[The NGO and the consequence they faced — 2–3 connected sentences]
[What changed, with ONE sourced number — the benefit, in program terms]
[ONE verbatim quote]
[A measured reflection that ties to the benefit (time/effort saved, reach at scale)]
[Soft question CTA]
#3–5 hashtags
```

**Template B — Customer story (StoryBrand in miniature).**
Hero (the NGO) → problem → the plan they followed on Glific → the transformation (one number) → the community detail. Glific named once, as guide.

**Template C — Sector insight.**
Open with a tension the sector knows ("Most awareness programs can't tell if the behaviour sticks after they leave"). Make the point. Land it on a Glific-enabled example. Earn the lesson; don't pitch.

## Worked example (Template A)

> One program lead. One WhatsApp number. Thousands of adolescent girls across 54 schools.
>
> Shraddha runs MukkaMaar, which builds self-defence and agency for adolescent girls. For a long time, every message went out by hand, and the reach of the program was capped by how many hours one person had in a day.
>
> She isn't a "tech person," and she didn't need to be. She designed the chatbot herself — voice notes, in Hindi and Marathi, responding in the language each girl actually speaks. That outreach now runs on its own, and reaches 3,000+ girls without adding a single staff member to send it.
>
> On the support behind her, she says: *"It feels like I have a team."*
>
> When outreach is no longer capped by how many messages one person can send by hand, a program's reach can grow with the need instead of the headcount.
>
> How is your team handling outreach as your numbers grow?
>
> #NonprofitTech #WhatsAppForGood #NGOIndia

*(Note the moves: a concrete program reality in the opener, no Glific name, no theatrical reversal; the NGO is the hero; one sourced number; the lead value is time saved and reach-without-headcount, not "join a community"; the community quote appears in proportion, as support; a calm, Sage-register close; one soft question CTA.)*

## LinkedIn validation (in addition to the three-layer pass)
- First two lines earn the read with a concrete program reality — not a theatrical hook or reversal?
- Glific absent from the opener and named at most once or twice?
- Leads with a benefit (not community-as-headline); exactly one idea, one number, one quote?
- Sage register: no setup-then-reversal, no dramatic fragments, no narrator asides?
- Body link-free (link in comment if needed)?
- Reads aloud like a thoughtful practitioner, not a brand account?
