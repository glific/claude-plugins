# Doc 2 — Messaging

The master messaging reference. Where copy decisions get made: how to articulate the value, the problem, and the solution; which differentiator earns the "why Glific"; and the brand lines. Read this for every asset.

**What we sell:** Glific is a **SaaS platform** — the AI-powered WhatsApp chatbot platform built for nonprofits. We lead with the product and the benefits it delivers. Community, open source, and the rest are *differentiators* that explain why an NGO chooses Glific — they support the sale; they are not the headline.

Rule of one: **one asset carries one idea and one proof point.** Two ideas means two assets.

## 1. The value spine (lead with this)

**The one-line north star (customer-facing):**
> Glific helps nonprofit teams use WhatsApp effectively to reach their program goals at scale.

Value is articulated in two tiers. Lead with a **primary benefit**; bring in a **secondary benefit** as the supporting "and." Don't recite all of them — pick the one that matches the audience and the asset.

### Primary benefits (the headline value)
1. **Time and effort saved.** Programs stop running outreach by hand. The work that took a person hours — sending messages one by one, chasing follow-ups, tracking engagement across groups — runs automatically.
2. **Not limited by scale.** A program team's time is already spoken for. Every hour spent on manual WhatsApp work — sending messages one by one, chasing follow-ups, managing group broadcasts — is an hour not spent connecting with beneficiaries or improving the program. Glific automates the manual operations so the team's time goes back to program work, and the reach goes to thousands of beneficiaries in their own language, at the time they need. The ceiling that lifts is the capacity ceiling on reach — not a headcount ceiling. *(Never frame this as "without growing the team," "without an extra hire," or any headcount-reduction language. See positioning.md §8.)*

### Secondary benefits (the supporting value)
1. **Every team member can run it.** A program lead with no technical background designs, builds, and runs flows themselves — not just the one "tech person." The whole team can build and improve programs.
2. **Two-way conversations with beneficiaries, on the channel they already use.** WhatsApp lets the program actually converse with beneficiaries — and Glific solves what makes that hard at scale: language (multilingual, voice), context (AI-driven, responds to what was said last), and volume (automated, personalised). Plus: every conversation becomes data the program can report on.

## 2. Problem articulation

State the problem as an escalating consequence the program feels — never as a feature gap. This is the opening move for most assets. (From the deck and the overview video — use this shape.)

> Every nonprofit already uses WhatsApp. Their beneficiaries are there; the conversations happen there; program teams are already showing up — every day, on one phone, for thousands of people.
>
> One team. Thousands of beneficiaries to reach — in the language they speak, at the time they need.
> Every unanswered message is a missed intervention. Every missed intervention is impact delayed.

**The deeper problem behind the capacity problem:** the program runs a high-touch model and wants to keep the human feel while reaching far more people — and broadcast messaging doesn't drive behaviour change. The real emotional entry point is *"we want to reach everyone properly, and we don't know how to do it at this scale."*

**Problem angles to draw from (pick one):**
- **Capacity / time:** one team, one phone, thousands of people; outreach by hand hits a ceiling the moment the program grows.
- **Scale without losing the human feel:** broadcast messaging is one-way and impersonal; it doesn't change behaviour.
- **Language & context:** beneficiaries speak many languages and need a response in theirs, in their context — hard to do by hand.
- **Starting:** a high-touch field program wants to digitise but has no technical team and no idea where to begin.
- **Reporting:** engagement is scattered across phones and groups; pulling it into a funder report takes a manager hours.
- **Cost confusion:** layered costs make NGOs underestimate total cost and feel misled after converting.

## 3. Solution articulation (what it does)

State the solution as relief from the chosen problem, in program terms. The platform is the *how*; the benefit is the *what*.

**The one-sentence solution:**
> Glific removes the ceiling: automated, personalised, multilingual, AI-powered conversations at program scale — with conversational insights that help programs grow their impact.

**How it works (the plain three-step, from the video):**
1. **You design the conversation** — by text or voice, in any language.
2. **You build the flow once, automate it, and keep improving it.**
3. **Every conversation becomes data you can report on** — conversational insights for transparent impact reporting.

**A concrete moment to make it real (use this kind of detail, not a feature list):**
> A student sends a voice note in Marathi. Glific understands it, responds in her language, and moves the conversation forward.

**The four-step program journey (when an asset needs the fuller arc):** identify & build → pilot & iterate (≈50 beneficiaries) → analyse → engage. (Use for onboarding/how-it-works content.)

**Capabilities — only ever as proof of a benefit, never as a naked list:** text + audio outreach, multilingual & voice flows, intuitive flow builder, WhatsApp group automation, WhatsApp forms, voice-to-text, Google Sheets / BigQuery integration, aggregated insights, 10+ sector templates.

## 4. Differentiators — why Glific (with Reasons to Believe)

These answer "why Glific over Wati, Turn.io, a custom app, or manual WhatsApp." They support the sale; they don't replace the benefit. **Community is an important differentiator — one of several, not the headline.** Lead an asset with the differentiator the audience's objection calls for; never stack two.

Each has **the challenge**, **the value**, and the **Reason to Believe** (pull the specific number/quote from `proof-library.md`).

### D1 — Built for nonprofits
- **Challenge:** the category's tools (Wati, Engati, Gupshup) are built for enterprise or e-commerce; they assume a tech-fluent team and know nothing about program work.
- **Value:** Glific is designed for how nonprofit programs actually run and how beneficiaries actually communicate — voice, multilingual, 10+ sector templates.
- **RTB:** 120+ NGOs across 10+ sectors; field-built sector templates; "every decision starts with the NGO."

### D2 — A community of nonprofits *(important differentiator)*
- **Challenge:** most platforms onboard you and leave; on Turn.io, a customer said, "after we onboard, we are on our own."
- **Value:** with Glific you join the nonprofits who came before you — a peer network that shares what works, plus cohorts, sprints, and meetups, and a roadmap shaped by NGOs.
- **RTB:** the 120+ NGO network; cohort learning; 2-day sprints; offline meetups; co-creation. Anchor quote: *"It feels like I have a team."* (Shraddha, MukkaMaar.)
- **Discipline:** frame as a genuine differentiator (peer network that compounds), and keep it proportionate — it strengthens "why Glific," it doesn't replace "what Glific does." We sell the SaaS.

### D3 — AI-powered personalisation in any language
- **Challenge:** bulk messages are one-way and impersonal; 1:1 feels impossible without more staff; beneficiaries speak many languages.
- **Value:** context-aware, AI-driven flows that respond to what a beneficiary said last, in their language — a 1:1 feel at scale.
- **RTB:** multilingual + voice flows; AI-led contextual conversations; the AI accelerator cohort.

### D4 — Open source, full data ownership, Digital Public Good
- **Challenge:** for NGOs in sensitive sectors (health, rights, child protection), vendor-held data is a compliance and trust risk.
- **Value:** Glific is open source; the NGO owns its data; meets DPDP and GDPR; integrates with BigQuery for funder-ready reporting.
- **RTB:** open-source codebase (no lock-in); recognised Digital Public Good; DPDP + GDPR; BigQuery.

### D5 — Transparent, nonprofit-focused pricing
- **Challenge:** layered cost structures create confusion; NGOs underestimate total cost and feel misled after converting.
- **Value:** a simplified, transparent cost narrative framed in cost-per-beneficiary terms.
- **RTB:** **one platform fee, no feature tiers, unlimited users (no per-seat)** — every plan includes everything; only usage-based messaging is added on top; itemised additional costs are published; comparison framed against manual operations first, then Wati/Turn.io. ## 7. Pricing narrative

Own it before the NGO constructs one (positioning Finding 2). Principles:
- **Anchor against the status quo first.** The honest comparison is manual WhatsApp / a custom app / offline program cost — not a SaaS budget. Frame in program terms: cost per beneficiary, staff hours freed, data into the funder report.
- **Then compare against named alternatives** (Wati, Turn.io) transparently — total cost of ownership, not sticker price.
- **Be transparent about the layers** (platform, BSP messaging, setup, optional consulting) up front; post-conversion confusion is the churn risk.
- **Don't apologise for price, and don't discount as the reflex.** The job is worth-it, not cheaper. Pair any price with the benefit.

## 8. One story, everywhere

The risk this doc exists to kill: different teams describing Glific differently (website says "chatbot," a deck says "low-code builder," a founder says "communication platform"). The fix is discipline:
- The **descriptor** ("India's Most Preferred WhatsApp Chatbot Platform for Nonprofits") is the same everywhere.
- The **lead value** (time and effort saved; reach at scale) is the same everywhere.
- The **hero** (the NGO) is the same everywhere.
- The **problem** is told as a consequence, the same way, everywhere.
- **Community** appears in proportion — a strong "why Glific," never the whole pitch.

A channel changes the *format and the emphasis* — never the story. That's what the channel playbooks are for.
