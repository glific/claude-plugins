# Voice & Tone

How Glific sounds, regardless of channel. Check this before delivering any asset — the archetype, the register, and the banned list especially.

## Brand archetype: the Sage

Glific is a **Sage**. A Sage earns trust by helping people understand — not by hyping, urging, or performing. It values clarity, evidence, and truth, and it teaches rather than sells. Applied to Glific: we explain how something works and why it matters to a program, calmly and concretely, and we let the understanding do the persuading. We are the guide who has seen 120+ NGOs do this before and can say, plainly, what works.

What the Sage is **not**: a hype-man (no "supercharge," no "game-changing"), a showman (no dramatic reversals or cliffhangers), or an urgency salesman (no "now more than ever," no countdowns). Confidence here is quiet.

## The register (our model: Rohini Nilekani Philanthropies)

We write the way the best sector writing reads — **measured, essayistic, and explanatory.** Audit reference: Rohini Nilekani Philanthropies' reports. The qualities to emulate:

- **Measured and intellectually grounded, yet accessible.** Neither academic nor dumbed-down. It respects the reader.
- **Flowing sentences that build toward insight.** Medium-to-longer sentences, parallel construction, ideas that connect — rather than staccato fragments fired for effect.
- **Observation first, then the concrete.** Open by establishing a stance or a real situation, then ground it in specifics and evidence.
- **Hopeful, dynamic verbs; plain vocabulary.** Substance without jargon.
- **No dramatic reversals, no clickbait.** Understanding is built methodically, through framing, evidence, and invitation.

The discipline this asks of every channel: even where a feed or an inbox rewards a strong opening, earn attention with a **genuine observation or a concrete program reality** — never with theatrical hooks.

## Five voice principles

1. **Consequence before feature.** Name what a program faces before naming what Glific does. "Every unanswered message is a missed intervention" before "automated flows."
2. **Program language, not tech language.** Beneficiaries, interventions, outreach, field staff, funder reports, M&E, sectors, program goals. Never "users," "customers," "leads," "end-users."
3. **Specific beats grand.** A voice note in Marathi from someone who can't type beats "multilingual capabilities." One real NGO and one real number beat "organisations everywhere."
4. **Explain, don't exclaim.** Build the point in connected sentences. Trust the reader to follow an argument; you don't need a fragment to land it.
5. **Honest about the messy parts.** The sector isn't tidy; cost isn't simple; tech is intimidating. Say so plainly. Don't oversmooth.

## Register by audience

| | Operator (program lead) | Decision-Maker (founder/ED) |
|---|---|---|
| Opens with | their daily program reality | the scale challenge and the stakes |
| Proof that lands | a peer's plain-spoken experience | a peer's outcome, with the number |
| Tone | calm, practical, "here's how this works" | measured, evidence-led, "here's the credible path" |
| Avoid | jargon, feature dumps | inspiration without evidence |

## Banned phrases & constructions

A hit means **rewrite, not soften.**

**Narrative theater (the most important ban — it breaks the Sage register).** These are copywriter devices that perform instead of explain. Avoid all of them:
- **Setup-then-reversal:** "The mission was never the problem. The maths was:" / "The X was never the problem — the Y was." / "It works — until it doesn't." / "Turns out, it wasn't about Z at all."
- **Fragments fired for punch:** a one-line paragraph dropped for drama ("And that changes everything." "Every single one."). Use complete, connected sentences.
- **Narrator asides:** "Here's the thing." "What stayed with me was…" "Let that sink in." "But here's the kicker." "Plot twist:" "And honestly?"
- **The dramatic rhetorical question as a hook** ("What if I told you…").
This ban is *in addition to* the contrast ban below; a reversal is its sentence-level cousin.

**Contrast constructions (banned outright):** "X, not Y." "Not just X, but Y." "More than X, it's Y." State the positive directly. (Replace "It's not a chatbot, it's a community" with "A community of 120+ NGOs uses it.")

**Startup / SaaS hype:** leverage (as a verb), seamless, robust, best-in-class, world-class, cutting-edge, game-changing, revolutionary, transformative, paradigm, supercharge, unlock (as filler), turnkey, frictionless, next-gen, "powerful platform."

**Generic nonprofit / impact filler:** empower communities, drive impact, create change, make a difference, holistic solution, ecosystem of change, changemakers (as a label), "at the heart of everything we do." (Note: describing the benefit that *every team member can run Glific* is fine — just don't reach for the word "empower" to say it.)

**Hedged / weasel language:** designed to, aims to, helps to (as filler), may help, can potentially, strives to. State what it does.

**Staff-replacement language (hard stop — harmful and wrong):** "without one extra staff hire," "without growing the team," "no additional headcount," "reach more without hiring," "do more with fewer people," or any phrasing that implies Glific substitutes for a person or makes staff unnecessary. Glific's value is that staff time is freed from manual work and returned to real program work — the team matters more, not less. See positioning.md §8 for the full reasoning. A sentence that could be read as "Glific means you need fewer people" must be rewritten.

**Banned openers:** "In today's landscape…", "In a world where…", "Now more than ever…", and opening a LinkedIn post or a hero with the word "Glific."

**Filler intensifiers:** genuinely, honestly, truly, simply, really, very, incredibly.

## Reference phrases (this is the sound)

On-brand lines — measured, concrete, declarative — drawn from the deck, the overview video script, and customer voice:
- "Every unanswered message is a missed intervention. Every missed intervention is impact delayed." *(parallel, measured — not a reversal)*
- "One team, one phone, for thousands of people."
- "Reach your beneficiaries where they are, when they need."
- "A student sends a voice note in Marathi. Glific understands it, responds in her language, and moves the conversation forward."
- "You design the conversation once, automate it, and keep improving it. Every conversation becomes data you can report on."
- "Trusted across education, health, and many other sectors. Open source, and a recognised Digital Public Good."
- "When you join Glific, you join the nonprofits that came before you."
- "Glific made it very easy for me." *(customer)*

## Quick self-check before sending
- Sage register: am I explaining, or performing? Any reversal, dramatic fragment, or narrator aside?
- Did I open with a real program reality or observation — not Glific's name, not a theatrical hook?
- Did I lead with the benefit (time and effort saved, reach at scale)? Is community used as a differentiator, not the headline?
- **Staff-replacement check:** could any sentence be read as "Glific means you need fewer people"? If yes, rewrite immediately.
- Is every number in `proof-library.md`?
- One idea, one proof?
- Any banned phrase or "X, not Y"?
- Read aloud: does this sound like a thoughtful practitioner, or a landing page?
