## 1. North Star & internal anthem

**Internal anthem (not customer-facing — the test every message must pass):**

> Glific helps NGOs use WhatsApp (WABA) **effectively** to meet **measurable program goals** — where *effectively* = at scale, personalised, contextual, human-like, cost-effective, AI-leveraged, reliable, and data-secure.

This is the north star Pranesh set across Glific. Customer-facing copy never says "WABA" or lists these eight adjectives — but every asset should be defensible against this sentence. If a draft promises something outside it, it's off-strategy.

**2027 goals that the story has to serve:**
- **Scale:** 200 NGOs onboarded.
- **One-stop chatbot solution:** simple, sticky experiences for new and existing customers.

**Two threats the narrative is built to blunt:**
- Big-tech competition (Meta/WhatsApp, Gupshup building into the NGO space).
- Funding dependency (donor priorities shifting).

A sharp, owned narrative is what makes both threats more manageable — it's why this work exists.

## 2. What Glific is

**Glific is a SaaS platform** — the AI-powered WhatsApp chatbot platform built for nonprofits. What an NGO gets has three parts, but the product we sell and lead with is the platform:

- **Platform (what we sell, what we lead with)** — the WhatsApp chatbot builder: flows, voice, multilingual, integrations, insights.
- **Program** — the guided journey from first use case to a running program (identify & build → pilot & iterate → analyse → engage).
- **Community** — the 120+ NGO peer network: cohorts, 2-day sprints, offline meetups, co-creation. An important **differentiator** (a reason an NGO chooses Glific), kept in proportion — never the headline. (See finding 3 and `messaging.md`.)

Customer-facing descriptor, lead form: **"India's Most Preferred WhatsApp Chatbot Platform for Nonprofits."** (A "& Community" variant exists for community-led assets only.)

## 3. Category & competitive frame

Glific sits in the **WhatsApp conversation-automation** space. The trap is letting the category flatten Glific into "another chatbot builder" — most players in the category are built for enterprise teams or e-commerce, not nonprofits.

| Competitor | What they are | How they fall short for NGOs |
|-----------|---------------|------------------------------|
| **Turn.io** | Closest competitor; nonprofit-oriented | Picking up bigger customers; onboards you and then "you're on your own" — no compounding community. |
| **Wati** | WhatsApp engagement platform, SMB/enterprise | Built for commercial teams; no nonprofit program context; perceived cheaper at the sticker but no support ecosystem. |
| **Engati / Gupshup** | Enterprise/e-commerce chatbot & BSP platforms | Tech-fluent buyers assumed; no field-built sector templates; no community. |
| **Custom-built apps** | NGO builds its own | Expensive, slow, fragile, needs in-house tech the NGO doesn't have. |
| **Manual WhatsApp** | One staffer, one phone, a spreadsheet | The real status quo for ~90% of incoming NGOs. Hits capacity limits fast; doesn't scale. |

**Two truths about how NGOs actually evaluate:**
- **The frame of comparison is ₹0, not a SaaS budget.** Chatbot cost is almost never a line item; the alternative an NGO is weighing is *manual WhatsApp* or *a custom app* — not Wati's price. Lead cost conversations against the status quo (staff hours, cost per beneficiary, the funder report that doesn't get built), and only then against named competitors.
- **~90% arrive having done something manually on WhatsApp; very few have used a chatbot.** They trust WhatsApp as a channel; they do not understand the ISV/BSP/Meta ecosystem at all, and don't want to. Entry is usually word-of-mouth or senior-leadership endorsement.

## 4. Personas & their entry questions

Two people matter at the point of decision. Each walks in with a specific question. The job of copy is to answer *their* question, in *their* words, before talking about the platform.

### The Operator — non-technical program / M&E lead (primary user)
- Does the actual work: builds flows, runs the program, prepares the funder report.
- **Entry question:** *"I am not a techie. I want to reach 3 million girls. How simple can you make this for me?"*
- Buys when they believe they personally can run this — and that they won't be left alone when they get stuck.
- Voice: program language, not tech language. Relief, not features.

### The Decision-Maker — founder / ED / program director (primary economic buyer)
- Owns the mission and the budget; rarely touches the tool.
- **Entry question:** *"My cause has 3 million potential beneficiaries. Running this purely offline is not scalable — not at time, talent, or cost. How can Glific help me take my program online?"*
- Buys when they see a credible path from offline bottleneck to online scale, with proof from a peer NGO.
- Voice: scale, feasibility, time/talent/cost, evidence.

> A third audience — **funders / ecosystem partners** — matters for the mission but is **out of scope for the current channel playbooks** (LinkedIn, newsletter, website). Note it; don't build for it yet.

**The shared shift:** every NGO interview maps to the same move — from *"what is Glific"* to *"why should this NGO care."* When copy leads with what (chatbot builder, low-code, open-source), it erases differentiation, because every vendor says the same. When copy leads with the NGO's program reality, the NGO becomes the frame of reference. That shift is the whole positioning.

## 5. The three strategic findings

These come from Feb–Apr 2026 research (customer interviews, pre-sales shadowing, touchpoint audit, internal conversations). They are *why* the messaging is built the way it is.

**Finding 1 — The entry question is "why Glific," and the old story answered "what Glific is."**
NGOs arrive with a sharp problem (bulk messaging is broken, follow-ups fall through, manual WhatsApp doesn't scale). Leading with "chatbot builder / low-code / open-source" answers a question they didn't ask and that competitors answer identically. Lead with the program problem and the value; earn the right to describe the platform.

**Finding 2 — Pricing is a retention risk, not a discount problem.**
Glific is perceived as more expensive than alternatives. Existing customers stay partly because of switching friction and the peer community — fragile if competitors improve onboarding. The answer is **not** to drop price; it's to **own the pricing narrative** before the NGO constructs one: transparent, simplified, framed in program terms (cost per beneficiary, staff hours saved, data into the funder report), compared against manual operations first and Wati/Turn.io second.

**Finding 3 — Community is a real differentiator; the product still leads.**
Research surfaced support and the peer community as a genuine reason NGOs choose and stay with Glific — a customer who onboarded onto Turn.io said, "after we onboard, we are on our own." But Glific sells SaaS, and the decision (see SKILL.md) is to **lead with the platform and its benefits** (time and effort saved, reach at scale) and treat community as **one important differentiator among several**, held in proportion. Frame community as the peer network that compounds — cohorts, sprints, meetups, co-creation, 120+ NGOs — not a helpdesk (helpdesk support is table stakes; competitors will match it). The sentiment to evoke when community *is* the asset's angle: *"it feels like I have a team."* Just don't let it become the whole pitch.

## 6. StoryBrand narrative design

The NGO is the hero; Glific is the guide; the transformation is the proof. Every long-form asset (deck, video, case study, anchor blog) should map onto this arc.

| StoryBrand element | Glific's story |
|--------------------|----------------|
| **A character** | The NGO founder & M&E lead. |
| **with a problem** | Manual programs are a bottleneck at scale — and scale brings new problems (analysis, personalisation, data security). |
| **meets a guide** | Glific: *reach your beneficiaries where they are, when they need* — a platform that removes the capacity ceiling, with a peer community behind it. |
| **who gives a plan** | Identify & build → pilot & iterate → analyse → engage. Four simple steps. |
| **and a call to action** | *"See what your program looks like on Glific"* — contextualised by sector, size, use case. |
| **that ends in success** | A non-technical team's time freed from manual operations — back to connecting with beneficiaries, improving the program, and reporting with confidence — while the program reaches thousands, in their language, at scale. |
| **avoiding failure** | Beneficiaries you can't reach in their context; staff time lost to manual workflows; time/cost/talent capping your impact. |

## 7. Positioning statement

A four-part statement (borrowed structure: target → category → benefit → differentiation). Use it to keep any asset honest; it is not customer-facing copy as-is.

> **For** resource-constrained NGOs running programs at scale across India and East Africa, **who** depend on WhatsApp to reach beneficiaries but hit the limits of manual outreach, **Glific is** the WhatsApp chatbot platform built for nonprofits **that** lets a non-technical program lead build, run, and grow a program — freeing their team's time from manual outreach and reaching beneficiaries at scale, in their language. **Unlike** enterprise tools (Wati, Engati, Gupshup) or the nearest competitor (Turn.io) that hand you a platform and leave, **Glific** is built for nonprofit programs and backed by a 120+ NGO peer community.

## 8. What we are / what we are not

| We are | We are not |
|--------|-----------|
| A SaaS platform built for nonprofits, with a peer community behind it | A standalone chatbot tool you're left alone with |
| Built from the field, for nonprofit programs | An enterprise/e-commerce platform repurposed for NGOs |
| The guide; the NGO is the hero | The hero of our own story |
| Honest about cost, framed in program terms | The cheapest option (and we don't pretend to be) |
| Simple enough for a non-techie to run | "No-code magic" that hides what's really happening |
| Open-source, NGO owns the data | A black box that holds your beneficiaries' data |

When a positioning question isn't answered here, resolve it toward the product benefit (time/effort saved, reach at scale) and the hero (the NGO), with community as a differentiator in proportion — and flag it for the quarterly review.

## ⚠ Hard stop — the staff-replacement fallacy

**Never frame Glific as a tool that replaces people, reduces headcount, or removes the need to hire.**

Banned phrasing — any variant of:
- "without one extra staff hire"
- "without growing the team"
- "no additional headcount"
- "reach more without hiring"
- "do more with fewer people"
- any construction that implies Glific substitutes for a person

**Why this is wrong — and harmful:** Glific's problem articulation is about where staff time goes, not how many staff there are. A program team's time is already spoken for — every hour spent manually sending WhatsApp messages, chasing follow-ups, and managing group broadcasts is an hour not spent connecting with beneficiaries, learning from the field, or improving the program. Glific gives that time back. The team is still there; the team matters more, not less.

**The correct frame:** Staff time is being consumed by manual operations. Glific automates those operations so the same team can spend their time on meaningful program work — the actual reason they joined the organisation.

**Scale benefit, correctly stated:** Glific lets a program reach far more beneficiaries without the reach being capped by what one team can do by hand. The ceiling that lifts is the capacity ceiling on reach — not a headcount ceiling.

**Test before publishing:** If your sentence could be read as "Glific means you need fewer people," rewrite it. The answer is always: the same team, doing better work, reaching more people.
